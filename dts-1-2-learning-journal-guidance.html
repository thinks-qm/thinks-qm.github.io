K8.3

10th March 2026 – experienced pair programming

Pair programming for the first time! Challenging and eye-opening. We were sitting side by side sharing the same screen and keyboard. (I’ve heard some companies do pair programming remotely, but I haven’t got my head around that yet.) We swapped between driver and navigator roles every 20 minutes. At first, it felt awkward because I wasn’t used to explaining my thought process out loud while coding and it was strange to keep switching from writing code to observing. I realised how much I normally work in my own head. Having to talk through each step made me think more carefully about why I was doing things. It also helped me spot mistakes earlier because my partner would question things I overlooked. I learned that collaboration isn’t just about splitting tasks, it’s about sharing ideas and improving each other’s approach. I learned a lot when my partner was “driving” and by the end of the day, I already started to feel more confident in my own coding and explaining things logically when I was driving. Can see how it helps develop cleaner, more maintainable code. Guidance: Be open about things you learn about yourself and how you work, think, or learn... (metacognition)

Explainer: Pair Programming is when two developers work together at the same workstation, with one person writing the code (the "driver") and the other reviewing and guiding in real time (the "navigator"). The roles are typically swapped regularly so both people stay engaged. I have also learned about Mob Programming which is like pair programming but with the whole team — one person types while everyone else collaborates and contributes ideas together in real time.

K9.1

14th Feb 2026 - project manager handling uncertainty during a project restructure.

During a period where our team was told that project priorities were shifting but no firm details had been confirmed yet, I noticed how our project manager handled the ambiguity really well. Rather than waiting until everything was finalised, she called a short team meeting to acknowledge that things were uncertain, was honest about what she didn't know yet, and reassured us that she would communicate updates as soon as she had them. She also made a point of checking in with individuals one-to-one over the following days. At the time I didn't think too much of it, but reflecting back, this was a strong example of transparent and people-centred leadership. I feel like she prioritised psychological safety over pretending to have all the answers, which kept the team calm and focused. It could have been a lot more stressful than it was. It made me realise that good leadership isn't always about having the answers.

K9.2

6th April 2026 - leadership theories and styles

Recent lecture notes:

Transformational leadership focuses on inspiring and motivating people through a shared vision, whereas Transactional leadership is more focused on structure, rewards, and performance targets

Situational leadership - there is no single "best" style and that effective leaders adapt their approach depending on the competence and confidence of the individual they're leading

Servant leadership flips the traditional hierarchy, with the leader seeing their primary role as supporting and enabling their team.

Reflection: Having thought about this, I think I most identify with a mix of Transformational and Servant leadership. In my current role, I naturally tend to focus on understanding what people need to do their best work and trying to remove blockers for them, which aligns with the Servant model. But I'm also motivated by the idea of working towards a bigger purpose, which draws me towards the Transformational style. I'm less drawn to a purely Transactional approach, though I can see its value in structured environments where clear targets and accountability are important. Guidance: Your personal perspective is important.

K9.3

3rd September 2026 – reflections on a difficult team situation

I reflected on this event using Gibbs: Guidance: Use a variety of reflective models where you feel they will help you get the most out of a significant experience (whether it is seemingly negative or positive)

Description: During a recent project, our team was working to a tight deadline but there was no clear owner for a key deliverable. Two senior colleagues had different views on how it should be approached and neither stepped up to make a final call, which left the rest of the team unsure whose direction to follow.

Feelings: I found the situation quite frustrating, and I noticed it was affecting the team's confidence too. I wasn't sure whether it was my place to say anything as I’m still a junior member of the team, but I felt uncomfortable just watching things stall.

Evaluation: The lack of decisive leadership had a real impact. Work was being duplicated and the atmosphere in stand-ups became tense. On the other hand, it was a useful situation to observe because it highlighted exactly what can go wrong when accountability isn't clearly defined.

Analysis: Reflecting on this, I think the root issue was that neither colleague felt fully empowered to lead, possibly because roles hadn't been clearly defined at the start of the project. Maybe they lacked confidence too. I don’t think they see themselves as “management” so this may have held them back from taking action. This links to what I'd learned about Situational Leadership - a good leader reads the room and steps up when clarity is needed, even if it's uncomfortable.

Conclusion: Leadership isn't always a formal title, sometimes it means being the person who brings a bit of structure to a chaotic situation. I also recognised that I could have done more myself, for example by suggesting the team align on a decision-making process.

Action Plan: Going forward I want to be more proactive in flagging ambiguity early, and as I build my confidence, I want to practise helping others achieve agreement (consensus?) in situations where there's no clear lead. I will talk to my line manager about this and ask for further advice on how I can communicate more effectively in different situations so I can start to demonstrate leadership in small ways.
